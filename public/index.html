What it does

Reads what's there. Knows what's next.

Six listening modes calibrated to the work in front of you. Ten broadcast and streaming standards you can measure against. A reference library that knows the records that already translate. And a conversation that remembers what it just heard.

Tonal balance

A spectral read across the full range, weighted to genre and reference. See where the low‑mids are crowded, where the air is missing, and what to nudge. In dB, not adjectives.

Dynamics & loudness

Integrated LUFS, true peak, and crest factor, calibrated against streaming targets. The pearl meter shows compression character at a glance, not just a number.

Stereo image

Phase, correlation, and width over time. Catches the moment a chorus pulls left, or a synth pad collapses to mono on a phone speaker, before your reference listener does.

+0.18 corr112°center stable

Issue detection

Sibilance on a soloed vocal. A resonant peak around 380 Hz. Inter‑sample peaks above 0 dBTP. Listen Buddy flags them with a frequency and a suggested move.

⚠ 5.6 kHz sibilance⚠ +0.3 dBTP

Reference matching

Drop in a track that already translates. Listen Buddy maps its tonal curve, dynamics, and stereo profile onto yours, and tells you the gap. Not "close enough", but "−1.4 dB low‑shelf at 80".

Mix mode · listening to oleo_RVG_velvet.wav
You said the chorus felt “crowded.” Crowded how? I keep hearing it, can’t place it.
Maya · follow-up on last analysis
Three things are sharing the same shelf. The lead vocal sibilance, the snare top, and the hi-hat are all parked between 5–7 kHz. None of them are loud on their own. Together they make a wall.
When I called it crowded, I meant: nothing has its own room up there. The verse breathes because the hat drops out. The chorus doesn’t get that breath.
Listen Buddy
What if I leaned into it instead? Made the chorus feel deliberately busier than the verse?
Maya
That’s a real choice. A lot of records do exactly that. The trick is making it feel intentional, not accidental. Right now your chorus reads as “louder verse,” not “different section.”
If you’re going busier, give the listener a signpost: a tonal shift, a stereo widen on the hat, a synth pad arriving on the downbeat. Otherwise the energy lifts but the ear doesn’t know why it lifted.
Listen Buddy
Pad idea is interesting. What would you reach for?
Maya
Something that lives under the vocal, not next to it. A warm sine-ish pad sitting around 200–400 Hz, low in the mix, wide. It’d give the chorus weight without adding to the 5 kHz traffic jam.

About Zenphony

Built by people who make records.

Zenphony Audio was founded in 2025 by two musicians, producers, and composers who love every part of the craft. We track, write, mix, score, and master, and we build the tools we wished we had at 2 a.m. when the work is close and the room has gone quiet.

Made for working sessions

Built calm, by people who live in sessions. No flashy meters, no interruptions, no breaking the moment when an idea finally lands. Just steady ears for the long stretch.

Across the whole chain

We work in pop, R&B, film, and everything that bleeds between them. Tracking, production, mix, score, master. The tools we ship reflect that range, because the work does.

One product at a time

One thing, finished, before we start the next. Listen Buddy is the first, shaped in real sessions over real records. The next one is already taking shape in the room.
